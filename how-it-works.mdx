StackMap builds a living map of every service your application depends on. Rather than asking you to fill in a form, it gathers evidence from public signals — DNS records, WHOIS registries, SSL certificates — and combines that with any environment configuration you choose to share. The result is a graph that stays accurate as your infrastructure evolves.

## Onboarding methods

StackMap supports four ways to bring services into a project. Most users combine two or more of them to get comprehensive coverage.

## Domain discovery pipeline

When you submit a domain, StackMap dispatches a set of background jobs. These jobs run independently and in parallel, so the full scan completes in seconds rather than minutes.

### WHOIS lookup

StackMap queries the authoritative WHOIS registry for your root domain to retrieve registrar details, registration date, and — most importantly — the expiry date. This expiry date is stored and monitored so StackMap can alert you before your domain lapses.

### DNS record resolution

StackMap resolves all major DNS record types — `A`, `AAAA`, `CNAME`, `MX`, `TXT`, and `NS` — against public resolvers. Each resolved value is passed to the fingerprint database (described below) to identify which provider or service it points to. For example, a `CNAME` pointing to `*.vercel.app` is matched to Vercel; an `MX` record pointing to Google's mail servers is matched to Google Workspace.

### SSL certificate polling

StackMap connects to your domain and any discovered subdomains over HTTPS to retrieve the active TLS certificate. It records the certificate authority, the issued-to common name, and the `notAfter` expiry date. This expiry date feeds directly into expiry tracking alongside the WHOIS registration date.

## Service fingerprinting

Raw DNS and certificate data only tells you *what* a record says — the fingerprint database tells you *what service* it represents. StackMap maintains a curated database of provider signatures: hostname patterns, ASN ranges, TXT record prefixes, and certificate issuer strings that uniquely identify well-known cloud providers, CDNs, email services, analytics platforms, and more.

When a resolved record matches a fingerprint, StackMap creates a corresponding service node and labels it with the provider's name, logo, and category. Unmatched records are surfaced as generic nodes so you can manually assign them to a provider if needed.

## How the graph is assembled

Once all background jobs finish, StackMap assembles the graph by translating discovered data into nodes and edges.

**Nodes** represent individual services. Each node carries:

- The provider logo and display name
- Service type (e.g. DNS, hosting, email, analytics)
- Linked account or identifier where available
- Current operational status
- Expiry date, if applicable

**Edges** represent detected relationships between services. A relationship is inferred when one service directly references another — for instance, when your domain's `A` record resolves to an IP range owned by Cloudflare, an edge is drawn between the domain node and the Cloudflare node. Edges give you a clear picture of how services depend on each other, making it easier to reason about the blast radius of an outage or a migration.

The graph is rendered as an interactive canvas. You can drag nodes to create a layout that matches your mental model, zoom in to inspect a single service cluster, or zoom out to see the full picture.

## Expiry tracking

Infrastructure expiry is easy to miss. Domain registrations renew annually, TLS certificates expire every 90 days or 12 months depending on the issuer, and API keys or OAuth tokens often carry their own expiry windows.

StackMap tracks expiry dates from three sources:

1. **WHOIS data** — domain registration expiry
2. **SSL certificate polling** — TLS certificate `notAfter` date
3. **Manual entry** — expiry dates you provide when adding a service by hand

Scheduled background checks re-poll these sources at regular intervals to detect renewals and catch any changes. When a service's expiry date falls within a configurable alert window, StackMap generates an alert that appears on the service node in the graph and in your project's alerts feed.

<Note>
  **How .env parsing protects your secrets.** When you submit a `.env` file, all parsing happens locally in your browser — the raw file is never uploaded. StackMap's client-side parser extracts only the *key name* (e.g. `STRIPE_SECRET_KEY`) and uses it to infer the provider (e.g. Stripe). The corresponding value is discarded immediately and is never transmitted to StackMap's servers. You get full service discovery without exposing credentials.
</Note>
